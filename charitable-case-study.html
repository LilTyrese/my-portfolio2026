UX Case Study

Charitable: Simplifying Charitable Giving

A 4-person bootcamp project exploring how to reduce friction in online donations while building trust with verified charities. We conducted user research, usability testing, and iterated on the design over 8 weeks.

Team Size

Remote designers

UX/UI

Design & wireframes

The Problem

We started with an ambitious idea: help restaurants donate surplus food to charitable organizations. But early 2020 changed everything. Lockdowns prevented restaurant interviews, and our team went fully remote overnight.

So we pivoted. Instead, we asked: How can we help busy, generous people donate confidently to verified charities without decision fatigue or payment friction?

Key Pain Points We Identified

Users didn't know which charities to trust. They couldn't visualize impact. Payment forms felt long and security badges were unclear. Tax deductibility wasn't obvious.

User Research

We interviewed potential donors and identified two core personas:

Persona A

The Busy Professional

Wealthy and generous, but time-constrained. Wants to give but struggles with which charity to pick. Gets decision fatigue.

Persona B

The Conscious Giver

Wants to donate but needs reassurance. Concerned about legitimacy, overhead costs, and whether the money truly helps.

Design Solutions

We mapped each pain point to a specific design decision:

Problem

Too many charity choices

Users faced analysis paralysis choosing between dozens of organizations.

Solution

Curated categories first

Start with cause categories (Food, Housing, Education) so users pick a problem type before selecting specific charities.

Problem

Can't see impact

Donations feel abstract. Users don't know what their money actually does.

Solution

Impact preview

Show "$25 = 75 meals" before payment. Tangible outcomes build confidence.

Usability Testing

We tested with 3 participants: 1 instructor (expert review) + 2 mid-income professionals. Here's what we learned:

  • What worked: The donation flow was clear and straightforward. All 3 participants understood how to donate and felt confident in their decision.
  • What didn't: The onboarding felt too long with clunky circular icons. First impressions need to be tighter and more polished.
  • Key insight: Wealthy donors weren't the only audience. Mid-income givers had the same trust needs, and lower-income users might face affordability barriers we didn't test.

2020 Initial Wireframes

Low-fi flows mapping the user journey: Onboarding → Category Selection → Charity Detail → Donation Form → Success.

2026 Update: What I'd Do Differently

Six years later, I revisited this project with fresh perspective. Here's what I'd improve:

  • Trust-focused colors: Switch from coral to forest green. Trust is more important than warmth in financial flows.
  • Realistic donation amounts: $10–$100 instead of $5–$15. More aligned with actual giving patterns.
  • Multiple charities: Let users support 2–3 causes in one session, not just one.
  • Tax messaging: Emphasize immediate tax-deductibility upfront. It's a major decision driver.
  • Donation history: Add a profile feature showing annual giving, tax receipts, and cumulative impact.
  • Tighter onboarding: Remove clunky icons, streamline messaging. First impression matters.
  • Expanded testing: Include lower-income users. Affordability concerns are different pain points than trust concerns.
